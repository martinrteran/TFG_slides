    ABSTRACT

    Near-field radiative heat transfer has been a subject of great interest due to the applicability to thermal management and energy conversion. In this letter, a submicron gap between a pair of diced fused quartz substrates is formed by using micromachined low-density pillars to obtain both the parallelism and small parasitic heat conduction. The gap uniformity is validated by the optical interferometry at four corners of the substrates. The heat flux across the gap is measured in a steady-state and is no greater than twice of theoretically predicted radiative heat flux, which indicates that the parasitic heat conduction is suppressed to the level of the radiative heat transfer or less. The heat conduction through the pillars is modeled, and it is found to be limited by the thermal contact resistance between the pillar top and the opposing substrate surface. The methodology to form and evaluate the gap promotes the near-field radiative heat transfer to various applications such as thermal rectification, thermal modulation, and thermophotovoltaics.

    In this letter, we experimentally demonstrate submicron gaps between polar materials with the spacer conduction suppressed to the level of the radiative heat transfer or lower. Micromachining technique allows us to create spacer pillars sparsely, thereby suppressing the net spacer conduction. The uniform gap is realized by the exertion of a relatively large pressure, which compensate the non-ideality of the wafer surface. The gap value is evaluated by the optical interferometry at the four points of the substrates, which assures the parallelism and the uniformity of the gap. We set three pillar heights of 0.5 μm, 1.0 μm, and 2 μm. The heat fluxes across the gap are measured and modeled as the sum of the radiative heat flux and the parasitic conduction through pillars.

To take advantage of the unique characteristics of near-field radiative heat transfer, the parasitic heat conduction across the gap needs to be suppressed. We assume that the gap is filled with a spacer material and calculate the heat conductions through such materials with several thermal conductivities, as shown in Fig. 1. A material with thermal conductivity lower than 10−5 W m−1 K−1 is required to suppress the parasitic conduction to the radiative transfer or less. The required conductivity is three orders of magnitude less than the thermal conductivity of an architectural fabric for thermal insulation (typically around 0.02 W m−1 K−1 (Ref. 3737. A. M. Papadopoulos, Energy Build. 37, 77 (2005). https://doi.org/10.1016/j.enbuild.2004.05.006)). Thus, such a low conductivity is not sought for by choosing an appropriate spacer material but by engineering the pillar structures of low density. The film surface differs from the ideal flat condition in reality, by the presence of thermal stress, residual stress, or bow and warp of the wafer, to name a few. To compensate such nonideality and to realize a uniform gap, we exert a pressure from the top of a diced substrate stack as illustrated in Fig. 2(a). The pillars are expected to be tolerant to the applied pressure, thus the compressive strength of the pillar material should be large. We choose fused quartz for the moderately low thermal conductivity and the large compressive strength. Fused quartz has extra advantages such as surface resonance in the midinfrared, matured polishing technologies to obtain the flat surface, and availability of high-purity samples.

    The pillars are manufactured by the conventional micromachining processes on high-purity fused quartz substrates. A deep-ultraviolet contact photolithography and wet-etching with buffered hydrofluoric acid allow us to obtain trapezoidal pillars with controlled heights and densities, as depicted in Fig. 2(b). The rate of the wet-etch is calibrated at 23 °C to manufacture the pillars with three different heights. Dummy patterns are implemented for height measurements by a stylus profiler (KLA-Tencor, P-6) and measured to be 0.50 μm, 0.99 μm, and 1.98 μm. The pillars are placed at intervals of 1 mm, thus the dilution rate of the spacer material is 0.8 × 10−5 when 2 μm height pillars of 2 μm × 2 μm in the top area are used. The pressure applied to the top of the pillars is around 800 MPa with the exertion of a 1 N force, which is smaller than the compression strength of fused quartz (1.1 GPa). Due to the small pillar density, the chance of the interference between the pillars on the upper and the lower substrates is negligible. The substrate is diced into 19 mm × 8.6 mm to fit the measurement apparatus. The flatness of the diced substrates is evaluated by a 3D optical surface profiler (Zygo, NewView 5022). The substrates are locally smooth (Ra = 0.5 nm) and globally bending (±200 nm±200 nm). We simulate the global deformation of the substrate by using a commercial finite element method (FEM) simulator (COMSOL Multiphysics) and find that the applied force of 1 N is enough to compensate the intrinsic global bending. The local distortion between pillars is simulated to be as small as 10 nm under a 1 N force, thanks to the thick substrates (600 μm).
    The apparatus utilized in Ref. 3838. K. Ito, N. Kazutaka, H. Iizuka, and H. Toshiyoshi, Appl. Phys. Lett. 105, 253503 (2014). https://doi.org/10.1063/1.4905132, which was designed to evaluate the radiative heat flux between two parallel plates in the far-field regime, is redesigned for this study as shown in Figs. 2(c) and 2(d). The heat flux from the heater to the thermoelectric device is measured by the heat flux sensor based on thermopiles (Concept Engineering, FM-060). The output voltage is linear to the temperature difference between the top and the bottom of the sensor plate. The temperature of the thermocouples, TH and TL, is controlled by the heater and the thermoelectric device to enable a steady-state measurement. The apparatus is evacuated to an environmental pressure of lower than 5 × 10−3 Pa by a turbo molecular pump, thus the conductive transfer through rarefied air is negligible as inferred from Fig. 1. For the correct evaluation of the heat transfer, the sensitivity of the heat flux sensor is calibrated by utilizing the radiative heat flux between fused quartz substrates in the far-field regime, as reported in Ref. 3838. K. Ito, N. Kazutaka, H. Iizuka, and H. Toshiyoshi, Appl. Phys. Lett. 105, 253503 (2014). https://doi.org/10.1063/1.4905132. As for further calibration of the apparatus, thermal series resistance (RS), which corresponds to the temperature drop between the thermocouple and the substrate surface, is evaluated to characterize the measurement target (RT), which is the thermal resistance between the two substrate surfaces (Fig. 4(c)). We measured the heat flux through a single substrate to evaluate RS, which is retrieved to be 4 × 10−3 m2 KW−1 for the measurement condition. To exert and monitor the force, a compression spring and a load cell are installed. Two extension springs with relatively weak spring constant are installed on the side to realize a soft landing of the gap. For the evaluation of the gap, we utilize an optical fiber (Ocean optics, FL-400) to implement the optical interferometry in our apparatus. The incident white light and the reflected light propagate through the same fiber, which is realized by an optical setup with lenses and a half-mirror. Four holes are opened through the copper heat spreader as shown in Fig. 3 to sequentially insert the fiber.

    Typical spectra obtained by the optical interferometry are shown in the black curves in Fig. 3. Dips and peaks in the envelope are seen. The calculated reflection spectra by the Fabry-Perot equation are also shown in Fig. 3. The position of dips and peaks agrees well between the measured and the calculated spectra. The variation of the estimated gap values at the four holes is evaluated to be smaller than 100 nm by the standard deviation, which indicates a uniform gap without the inclusion of dust particles. The gap is slightly smaller than the pillar height possibly because of the deformation of the pillar top as seen in the lower right SEM image of Fig. 2(b).
    Radiative heat transfer across the gap is evaluated in a steady-state condition. Temperature biases of 5 K, 10 K, 15 K, and 20 K are applied between two thermocouples while TL is kept at 293 K. The output voltage of the heat flux sensor is monitored and is converted to heat flux using the calibrated sensitivity. The temperature difference between two substrate surfaces ΔT is calculated from the heat flux and RS. The relationship between ΔT and the heat flux is plotted in Fig. 4(a) with theoretically calculated heat fluxes. In this small temperature range, the relationship between ΔT and the heat flux is approximated as linear, and the slope is treated as the heat flux coefficient in the following discussion. The heat flux coefficients from the measurements, which are repeated four times for each nominal gap, are shown in red dots in Fig. 4(b). It is clear that the measured heat transfer coefficients are almost equal to or smaller than twice of the theoretical prediction, which indicates that we suppress the parasitic spacer conduction to be not greater than the radiative heat transfer.

    Finally, we modeled the heat transfer between two surfaces using an equivalent thermal circuit model shown in Fig. 4(c). The equivalent circuit consists of the radiative resistance RR, the geometrical resistance of the pillar RG, and the contact resistance between the pillar top and the surface of the opposing substrate RC. The geometrical resistance is simulated by FEM, using the geometrical parameters of the trapezoidal pillar retrieved from SEM image as shown in Fig. 4(d). The contact resistance has been reported to vary on the material hardness, surface roughness, pressures, and so on,3939. E. G. Wolff and D. A. Schneider, Int. J. Heat Mass Transfer 41, 3469 (1998). https://doi.org/10.1016/S0017-9310(98)00067-2 thus it is used as a fitting parameter. The resulting thermal contact resistance per area is 4 × 10−6 m2 KW−1, which is in the same order of magnitude as the reported thermal contact resistance of the micro-mechanically actuated films.4040. W. B. Song, M. S. Sutton, and J. J. Talghader, Appl. Phys. Lett. 81, 1216 (2002). https://doi.org/10.1063/1.1499518 The geometrical and contact resistances for one pillar are shown in Fig. 4(e). The contact resistance is larger than the geometrical resistance, thus the contact resistance plays a key role in the parasitic suppression. The modeled radiative transfer, which corresponds to RR, is retrieved from the measured heat transfer coefficient by subtracting the modeled pillar conduction, as shown in the green circles in Fig. 4(b). The agreement between the modeled radiative heat transfer and the theoretical prediction verifies the validity of our measurement and modeling. Furthermore, the model suggests further optimization of the pillars to suppress the pillar conduction; minimizing the top area of the pillar may help us to increase the contact resistance.
    In conclusion, we formed a submicron gap with the micromachined pillars made of fused quartz, and the gap was validated to be uniform by the optical interferometry. The heat transfer across the gap was measured and modeled, and the parasitic pillar conduction was suppressed to be below the amount of the radiative heat transfer. The further optimization of the pillars based on the equivalent thermal circuit model will eliminate the parasitic heat conduction. This gap formation and evaluation methodology can be applicable to other thermal sources including waste heat, and open prospects for the experimental research and development in the field of near-field radiative heat transfer.
